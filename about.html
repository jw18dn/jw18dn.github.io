Motivation and Biography

About Me

As a scientist I primarily focus on improving the abilities of legged robots. My passion is to take on the most interesting and complex problems related to building and controlling electromechanical systems. With a doctoral degree in mechanical engineering, the majority of my work focuses on developing robotic control algorithms and implimenting them in C++ and/or python. In addition, I occasionally assist in the design and testing of subassemblies which are then integrated into the next generation robotic platform. Since my departure from academia in December of 2023 my focus has been on developing solutions to tackle the real world problems legged systems encounter.

Professional Biography

Jason White started his career with a B.S. in civil engineering from Virginia Tech. After working as a civil engineer, structural engineer, and construction manager he decided to change careers from site development and construction management to electromechanical systems. He pursued and received his Ph.D. from Florida State University, where hisdissertation focused on "Real-Time Planning and Control of Dynamic Robots in Dynamic Environments." His research spans model-predictive control, dynamic obstacle avoidance, and control of bipedal robots.

During his time at Florida State University, he worked in the Optimal Robotics Lab under Dr. Christian Hubicki. His work has been published in top robotics venues including ICRA, IROS, and Humanoids. His contributions include novel approaches to real-time motion planning with integrated avoidance using quadratic programming, online gait optimization in resistive media, and through-contact locomotion generation via model-predictive control.

Upon graduation he joined Ghost Robotics where he has continued his work as a controls engineer. Examples of the projects he has contributed to include improvements to the robots mobility (for both blind and vision locomotion), control algoirthms for a 6 DOF manipulator, R&D work pertaining to the next generation actuator assembly, and numerous code refactors.
